I Turned My Knowledge of Women Into
A University Lecture.

No, this is not a red-pill rabbit hole. It's a genuine attempt to help clueless men understand the people they keep misreading.

S
@seangeraaald · Head Lecturer, Hougang University Product of a female-dominated household. Reluctant field researcher.

Welcome to Hougang University. We are happy that you joined us in the hopes of understanding women better. We believe in co-existence — whether you are part of the LGBT community or not, you have a place here. We believe in free speech.

Let me set the scene. I grew up in a female-dominated household. I have spent an unreasonable amount of my adult life in deep conversation with women — studying patterns, asking questions others were too proud to ask, and slowly building what I can only describe as a manual. So when I looked around at the men in my life — the gym bros, the computer science students whose only female interaction is a chatbot, the Reddit user, the men whose entire understanding of women comes from being in a friend zone or watching a romcom — I thought: maybe it's time to do something about this.

So I did what any reasonable person would do. I created a seminar. I gave it an academic name for the fun of it. I made slides, and I called it Introduction to Women. No, this isn't a dating advice seminar. It is not pickup artistry either. It is a genuine attempt to translate the way women think, feel, and operate — especially for men who have been operating on faulty assumptions their entire lives.

"Most men think they're helping by doing what was asked of them. But asking is a task itself."

First, Unlearn the Rules

I opened with one deceptively simple question: What is one "rule" you were told about women that you've found to be completely false? Every man has been carrying at least one bad theory. "Women are irrational." "If she's calm, everything is fine." Something absorbed so slowly from culture that it was never once examined out loud.

The whole point of starting here is this: before you can understand someone, you have to first clear out the noise. The misinformation. The shortcuts. The rules that were handed to you by other people who also didn't know what they were talking about.

Concept 01

Contextual Thinking vs. Linear Thinking

Here's a scenario I created. Your partner is upset because a friend forgot their birthday. A man thinking linearly processes this as: it's just a birthday, they were busy, move on. Clean. Simple. Plain wrong.

Contextual Thinking in action

The birthday isn't the issue. The birthday is a data point. This friend has been distant for months. The birthday is simply the latest entry in a long record of declining effort. To understand why someone is upset, you have to understand the full story — not just the news headline.

Women, broadly speaking, think in webs. Everything is connected to something else. A single incident is never isolated — it sits inside a larger emotional map that has been quietly maintained and updated over time. The linear thinker sees one event. The contextual thinker sees a pattern.

Neither is superior to the other. But if you're trying to connect with a woman who's upset, and you respond only to the surface event? You will consistently miss the actual conversation she's trying to have with you.

Concept 02

Mental Load: The Invisible Job Nobody Applied For

Mental load is the distinction between physical execution and cognitive ownership. If you put the groceries in the cart, that's execution. If you noticed three days ago that you were running low on oat milk, added it to a mental list, remembered to check whether you also needed detergent, and made sure to time the shop before the weekend guests arrived — that's cognitive ownership. One of those is visible. The other is not.

The person who knows where everything is in the house becomes the constant point of interruption. Every "where's the scissors?" is a tiny tax on their attention. Every "should we invite them for dinner?" is a decision that requires context they are already holding. Over time, this is exhausting in a way that doesn't look like exhaustion from the outside — because none of the individual moments seem like a big deal.

The problem isn't that men don't help. Most men, when asked, will gladly help. The problem is that asking is itself a task. If she still has to remember, plan, delegate, and follow up — she still feels like the manager. And the men? They're the volunteer assistant. The intern.

Look at three everyday tasks: grocery shopping, hosting friends, doing laundry. The visible actions are obvious. But the invisible work? Checking expiration dates, remembering a friend's allergy, knowing that someone needs a specific shirt clean for work on Monday. None of that shows up on the scorecard. All of it costs something.

Concept 03

Emotional Labor

Arlie Hochschild, a sociologist at UC Berkeley, coined the term "emotional labor" to describe the effort required to manage one's own feelings in order to influence the emotional state of others. She originally observed it in service workers — flight attendants, nurses — but it maps perfectly onto domestic and relational life.

In relationships and friendships, emotional labor shows up in three main forms:

  • Social Buffering Defusing tension between people, smoothing over awkwardness, keeping the group dynamic intact.
  • Validation Providing consistent empathy and active listening so that someone feels safe and genuinely heard.
  • Tone Policing Calculating how to raise a grievance in a way that won't immediately put the other party on the defensive.

That last one is particularly, extremely invisible. Every time a woman has sat with a frustration for hours, thinking about how to bring it up without a fight — that is labor. Genuinely draining labor.

The Method

The Traffic Light: A Framework for When She Vents

Perhaps the most practical tool I shared in the seminar. When a woman comes to you to vent or rant, don't immediately problem-solve or validate blindly. First, read the vibe. Then match your response to what's actually needed.

🟢 Green
Her VibeHighly emotional, low logic mode
She NeedsTo be heard and validated — not solved
You DoBe outraged on her behalf. Agree that the situation is genuinely problematic.
🟡 Yellow
Her VibeConfused, going in circles, needs input
She NeedsYour perspective — carefully
You DoOffer your view kindly. Don't make her feel foolish for not seeing it herself.
🔴 Red
Her VibeExhaustion. Burnout. Zero capacity left.
She NeedsSomeone to step in and handle it
You DoNothing. You act. You take over. You handle it — whatever "it" is.

The green zone is where most men fumble the hardest. They see a problem and immediately want to fix it — which reads as dismissive. She didn't ask you to fix the friend who forgot her birthday. She asked you to be outraged on her behalf for about 30 seconds. That's it. That's the whole job.

The Assignment

Every seminar ends with a real-world exercise. This one costs nothing except attention.

  • 1Pick one woman in your life — a sister, a mother, a colleague, a friend, or a partner.
  • 2Observe one piece of invisible labor she performs this week that you've always overlooked.
  • 3Acknowledge it — out loud, to her. Then notice how her vibe shifts.
